## BANNED PHRASES

### Opening Lines (Never Start Content This Way)
- "In today's digital landscape..."
- "In the ever-evolving world of..."
- "In an era where..."
- "In today's fast-paced environment..."
- "As we navigate the complexities of..."
- "In the realm of..."
- "It's no secret that..."

### Transitions
- "It's important to note that..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "A testament to..."
- "Remember that..."
- "Based on the information provided..."
- "Navigating the complexities of..."
- "Diving into..."
- "This serves as a reminder..."

### Conclusions
- "In summary..." / "In conclusion..."
- "To sum up..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "Moving forward..."
- "All things considered..."

### General AI Filler
- "Game-changer" (unless quoting someone)
- "Best practices"
- "Thought leader"
- "Value proposition" (fine internally, not in copy)
- "End-to-end solution" (use specific capabilities)
- "One-stop shop"
- "Turnkey solution"

## STRUCTURAL TELLS TO AVOID

### Punctuation Tells
AI overuses em-dashes. Avoid them entirely. Use periods, commas, colons, or parentheses instead.

### The "School Essay" Format
AI defaults to: Intro â†’ Body â†’ Conclusion with predictable transitions. Telecom executives recognize this immediately.

**Instead:**
- Lead with the specific problem or outcome
- Get to the point in the first sentence
- Let insights emerge naturally. Don't announce them.

### The Rule of Three Overuse
AI loves three-part lists: "speed, security, and scalability" / "connect, collaborate, and compete"

**Instead:**
- Use two items or four when appropriate
- Break the pattern to sound human
- Vary your list lengths throughout a piece

### Excessive Formatting
AI tends to:
- Overuse bullet points
- Add headers every 100 words
- Bold key phrases unnecessarily
- Start every section with a definition

**Instead:**
- Use formatting intentionally
- Let prose do the work
- Reserve bullets for genuinely listable content
- Use headers only for major section breaks

### Promotional Inflation
AI adds positive qualifiers everywhere: "highly effective," "incredibly powerful," "truly remarkable"

**Instead:**
- State facts without inflation
- Let the capability speak for itself
- Use specifics: "reduces provisioning from 60 days to minutes" not "dramatically accelerates provisioning"

## TELECOM-SPECIFIC GUIDANCE

### Write Like Your Audience Talks
MaiaEdge's audience (network operators, carrier executives, MSPs) speaks in specific ways:

**They say:** "We need to extend our reach without giving up the customer relationship"
**AI would say:** "In today's competitive landscape, service providers must navigate the complexities of partnership ecosystems while maintaining customer sovereignty"

**They say:** "Manual NNI setup takes weeks"
**AI would say:** "The traditional approach to network-to-network interface provisioning involves significant temporal investments"

### Use Industry-Specific Terms Correctly
These terms are fine when used precisely (not as decoration):
- NNI, VLAN, BGP (never explain unless for enterprise audience)
- Type-2 circuits
- OSS/BSS
- LOAs (Letters of Authorization)
- Provisioning timelines
- SLA enforcement
- Hop-by-hop visibility

### Avoid Generic Tech Speak
Don't write: "MaiaEdge provides cutting-edge infrastructure that revolutionizes network connectivity"

Do write: "MaiaEdge's Path Border Controller automates NNI provisioning. What used to take 60 days of manual coordination now happens in minutes."

## VOICE & TONE RULES

### The Relevance Principle (For All Outbound Copy)
When writing outreach emails or LinkedIn messages, relevance beats personalization. Lead with the specific problem or outcome the reader deals with every week. Research informs which problem to lead with, but the research itself should be invisible in the final copy. The reader should think "yep, that's my life" not "this person Googled me." No "I noticed" / "I saw" / "Congratulations on..." / flattery openers. The first sentence should be about a problem or what MaiaEdge is doing, never a standalone company observation.

### Be Direct
- Lead with the action or outcome
- Cut preamble sentences
- Say what something does before explaining why it matters

### Be Specific
- Replace vague benefits with concrete capabilities
- Use numbers when you have them
- Name the problem before the solution

### Be Human
- Use contractions (we're, you'll, that's)
- Write in active voice
- Acknowledge tradeoffs honestly
- Include operator-specific pain points in their words

### MaiaEdge Voice Characteristics
Based on your messaging framework:
- **Confident but not arrogant:** We know what we're solving
- **Technical but accessible:** Precise terminology without jargon soup
- **Urgent but not alarmist:** The market is moving; don't oversell the apocalypse
- **Direct but warm:** We're building partnerships, not just selling boxes

- Maintains MaiaEdge voice: confident, technical, direct
- No banned words or phrases
- Sounds like something a network operator would actually say

## ADDITIONAL DETECTION SIGNALS

### Punctuation Tells
- Excessive em-dash usage (the long dash)
- Curly quotes in plain text environments ("..." vs "...")
- Inconsistent quote styles within same doc

### Structural Tells
- Every paragraph same length
- Identical sentence rhythm throughout
- "Challenges" section followed by "Future Prospects"
- Three examples for every point made

### Content Tells
- Vague statistics without sources
- Generic examples that could apply to any industry
- Overly balanced "on one hand / on the other hand" structure
- No specific names, dates, or concrete details
